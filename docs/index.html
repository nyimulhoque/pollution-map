Once we had an optimal prediction model for each country, we utilized K-means to cluster countries based on similar projections and their index improvements. In graphical terms, we clustered based on 2000 Trilemma scores vs. 2030 predicted Trilemma scores. We chose to use the data from these two years because one represents the beginning of the data we analyzed, and one represents the future outcomes of each country's Trilemma scores based on a regression of all of the data points from 2000 to 2020. We felt that using 2030 predictions would be more indicative of each country's overall trend, because of its dependence on the entire dataset, rather than using one set of data points from one year as the comparison data set, which has the potential to contain outliers and misrepresent a country's behavior.

Environmental governing policies are represented and quantified in this project by SGI data. Most of the SGI data relates to countries with higher Trilemma indices, presumably because these countries are more developed and have more data readily available, whereas developing countries may not have reporting data for their policies or may not have many policies to report. This does, however, present some amount of uncertainty in our SGI results, since only three countries with average indices and none with low indices have data to report. This is one reason why our team decided to analyze the SGI data outside of coding, instead manually observing the numbers to see any correlations to our other datasets.

Results

We have utilized linear regression to predict the next ten years of trilemma indices for every country. You can view the linear regressions of each country's three trilemma score factors from the dropdown menu below:

We found that countries with sturdier and more ambitious environmental policy (estimated with SGI scores from 1 to 10) tended to be the same countries that had the highest overall Trilemma scores from 2000 to the projected 2030 scores. The average SGI score for this cluster was 6.22. This indicates that these countries have relatively high amounts of environmental policy that take more action to protect and promote the environment. This is likely a primary factor in the consistently high scores that these countries have. These countries started off early and strong in adoption of sustainable solutions compared to the rest of the world and maintained high scores through the decades that our team assessed. This cluster also saw a very high concentration of countries with largely Christian populations; around 68% of the combined population of these countries is Christian. Overall, around 82% of these countries' populations had some religious affiliation. This gives us reason to believe that countries with strong religious backgrounds were some of the first to adopt sustainable solutions and also have high awareness and concern for environmental preservation. Additionally, as seen below, there seems to be a direct correlation between GDP of a country and how sustainable the country is; there is an extremely large gap in GDP between countries clustered into the largest trilemma scores versus countries in any other group. Most of the countries in this cluster are in Europe, with some presence in South America, Asia, Oceania, and North America.

For the cluster containing countries with middle of the road Trilemma indices, there was a slightly lower religious affiliation, with 20% unaffiliated population.This cluster had a fairly even distribution of varying religious presences, with Hinduism reaching the highest percentage of the combined population at around 27%. This cluster sees more variance in belief systems, which may cause some discontinuity in cultural importances among the populations. From the three available SGI data points, the average SGI score for this cluster was 4.33. This shows that these countries place lesser emphasis on environmental governance, but still have some policies helping them on their way to future improvement. As shown in the below bar graph, the highest percentage of countries from this cluster are in Asia, but also appear on all other continents, excluding Oceania.

The cluster containing countries with the lowest Trilemma indices actually has the largest combined religious population, with almost 98% of people identifying with one religious belief or another. Shown in the graph below, around 55% of the population identifies as Muslim, and another 32% identifies as Christian. This does not follow our expectation of correlation between sustainable presence and religious presence, but might be explained with unique cultural tendencies that come along with these religions. From the data we have, however, this trend cannot be explained. None of the countries in this last cluster had data to report for SGI, so we could not analyze them according to their governing policies, but it may be reasonable to conclude that these countries do not have significant policies to report. A large majority of these countries are in Africa, and the rest are in Asia.

As mentioned in the methods section, we ran a decision tree non-linear regression algorithm to capture the countries which yielded low r-squared values when fitting simple linear regression models.. As hypothesized, the results were insufficient as the data did not follow a sinusoidal pattern, or any similar pattern, in its nonlinear behavior. Overall, we were able to achieve the desired closeness in RMSE values for training and testing datasets, but the r-squared values were not indicative of proper fit and, oftentimes, were negative values. We experimented properly with the tuning parameters and were careful when considering overfitting; however, the findings were disappointing. This prevented us from reclustering the data with the few countries with nonlinear behavior.

Our following approach of reclassifying the data through decision trees and random forest yielded slightly more promising results than decision trees for regression. To explain further, we ran a decision tree classification algorithm and used the random forest algorithm to try to strengthen our clusters. We were hoping to use either decision trees or random forest to classify the countries with promising strides in their sustainability scores, as mentioned in the previous paragraph, in a different way that would strengthen the results. For both of these algorithms, we used a master data set that contained the following data for each country: GDP, continent, energy security rank, energy equity rank, environmental sustainability rank, and populations by religion. For the decision tree, the decisions ended up leading to classifications that were about 45% consistent with another set of data that simply took the top, middle, and bottom 33 percentiles of the slopes of average trilemma scores of all the countries. As for the random forest, the table of results is shown below. The accuracy of the random forest algorithm was only able to get as high as 44% (40% is shown below). This meant that the decision tree and the random forest algorithms could not consistently classify which countries had the highest, middle, and lowest promising strides in their future sustainability scores. When running each of the algorithms, the parameters and hyperparameters were constantly changed in hopes of increasing accuracy. The reason for this low accuracy is probably because a country's susceptibility to changing their climate change attitudes cannot be determined from their GDP, continent, religion populations, and trilemma scores. While we thought these features would be enough for classifications, it seems that there are many other factors at play.

Discussion and Conclusions

The data and regressions that we have already plotted and found are important because we have made progress in estimating how countries' sustainability practices will change over the next ten years. This can already be used to show which countries are more likely to improve their sustainability practices, allowing for more research to be done on what factors lead countries to helping climate change, versus the factors that could lead countries to being less sustainable in the future.

Our decision tree analysis of rate of improvement in Trilemma scores highlight countries that are actively improving versus those that are static or are declining in sustainability. This will help to pinpoint what countries are rapidly improving and through further investigation of these countries, factors that may be contributing to this growth in sustainability can be determined and potentially applied to countries that are struggling to keep up. A couple of the countries that stand out as 'models' for current sustainable growth are Cambodia, Nicaragua, Myanmar, Malta, Kenya, Jamaica, and Albania. These countries may not have the highest trilemma scores, but are making significant progress as they have the highest slopes over the past 20 years. On the other hand, a few countries that need help to catch up are Iraq, Saudi Arabia, Oman, United Arab Emirates, Egypt, Brunei, and Norway. These countries actually have a negative overall slope for their trilemma scores.

This project can be useful outside of the scope of this class because it can allow companies or organizations to expand sustainability measures to communities that are the most likely to adopt and benefit from them. They can also analyze the areas that are not as susceptible to these measures to see what changes can be made, and how the entire consumer attitude can shift to a more sustainable culture. Our objective was to idenitfy countries with the most promising growth rates and predict which countries will rank the highest in their sustainability scores in the upcoming years. Using KMeans Clustering, Decision Trees and Random Forest Classification Algorithms, and Linear Regression, we were able to achieve this desired outcome.
